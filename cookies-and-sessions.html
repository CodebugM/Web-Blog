Cookies & Sessions

Cookies

Browser cookies are essentially pieces of information that are sent back and forth between a browser and a particular server.

Cookies are technically only name-value pairs, and, simplifying matters, are used to determine whether two requests came from the same server, for example, to keep a user logged in.

When a browser first connects to a particular server, i.e. sends a get request, to a particular server, the browser responds with a post request and a cookie, more specifically a Set-Cookie header.

The cookie is stored in the browser, and when the same browser connects to that server again at a later point (i.e. makes another get request) that cookie gets sent to the server inside a Cookie HTTP header. This information the server can then use in its post request (see example below).

Example:

The easiest example for cookie use is password storage. But a more interesting (also a slightly creepier) example is how online shopping sites like Amazon use cookies to monitor your (shopping) behavior on the site, even if you are not logged in.

When you open, say, Amazon and put something in your shopping cart without checking out, and then open other websites that use re-targeting ads (social media websites use those frequently) you will be reminded of the item that you wanted to buy to entice you to go back to your shopping cart there and complete check out.
